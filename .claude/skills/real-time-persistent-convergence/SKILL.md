# Real-Time + Persistent Convergence Strategy

Produce a product strategy for composing two fundamentally different world models — a real-time frame model (fast, implicit 3D, no export) and a persistent world model (slower, explicit 3D, full export pipeline) — into a coherent product experience. The output covers model complementarity mapping, convergence UX patterns, multi-model API design, pricing architecture, vertical-specific model routing, and updated competitive positioning.

This skill exists because World Labs now operates two world models with complementary strengths (RTFM and Marble). No general PM framework covers this — it requires reasoning about latency/quality tradeoffs, implicit vs. explicit 3D representations, and how multiple generation backends compose into a single user-facing product.

## Core Principles

1. **Real-time changes the unit of interaction from "request" to "experience"**
   "Software is eating the world, but AI is going to eat software. Every interaction becomes continuous, not transactional."
   — *Jensen Huang, CEO of NVIDIA* ([GTC Keynote, 2024](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y2F8yisiS6E))

   Marble operates in request-response mode: submit a prompt, wait minutes, get a world. RTFM operates in experience mode: interact with a world in real-time, explore continuously. This is not an incremental improvement — it changes the product category from "generation tool" to "interactive environment." Product strategy must account for both modes and design transitions between them. Users who experience real-time exploration will not accept minutes-long waits for every interaction.

2. **Products with multiple quality tiers need an explicit upgrade path**
   "The key to understanding freemium is that the free users are not the customers. They are the marketing."
   — *Josh Kopelman, Partner at First Round Capital* ([First Round Review](https://review.firstround.com/the-science-of-freemium))

   When two models offer different quality/speed tradeoffs, the product must make the upgrade path obvious and desirable. RTFM as "preview mode" and Marble as "production mode" creates a natural funnel: explore interactively (free/cheap), then render in high fidelity (paid). But this only works if the transition is seamless — if switching from RTFM to Marble requires re-starting from scratch, the funnel breaks.

3. **Disruption comes from below, even within your own company**
   "Disruption describes a process whereby a smaller company with fewer resources is able to successfully challenge established incumbent businesses. Specifically, as incumbents focus on improving their products and services for their most demanding customers, they exceed the needs of some segments."
   — *Clayton Christensen, The Innovator's Dilemma* ([HBS](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=46))

   RTFM is faster, lower-fidelity, and free (research preview). Marble is slower, higher-fidelity, and paid. If RTFM quality improves rapidly, it could cannibalize Marble's use cases from below — users who only need "good enough" worlds will stop paying for Marble. Product strategy must either differentiate the models on dimensions beyond quality (export, persistence, editability) or plan for convergence where Marble becomes the premium tier of a unified system.

4. **Spatial intelligence is the foundation, not the product**
   "The ability to understand, to reason, to interact with and to navigate the real 3D, 4D physical world is the foundation."
   — *Fei-Fei Li, CEO of World Labs* ([PYMNTS Interview, Jan 2026](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2026/fei-fei-li-says-ai-progress-now-depends-on-physical-context/))

   RTFM and Marble are both manifestations of spatial intelligence, not competing products. The product strategy should present them as capabilities of a unified spatial intelligence platform, not as separate tools. Users should think "I'm using World Labs' spatial intelligence" — whether they are in real-time exploration mode or high-fidelity production mode. The brand is the spatial intelligence layer; the models are implementation details.

5. **Content 3.0 requires real-time participation**
   "Content 3.0 is generated by you or together with you."
   — *Christoph Lassner, Co-Founder of World Labs* ([TED Talk](https://www.ted.com/talks/christoph_lassner_what_if_you_could_talk_to_your_favorite_character_in_a_movie))

   Lassner's Content 3.0 vision — where content is participatory, not passive — requires real-time generation. You cannot "participate" in content that takes 5 minutes to generate. RTFM makes Content 3.0 possible for the first time. This reframes RTFM not as a cheaper/faster Marble, but as the enabling technology for a fundamentally different kind of creative experience. Marble produces assets; RTFM produces experiences.

6. **Multi-product companies must avoid the "strategy tax"**
   "A strategy tax is an intentional decision to accept a suboptimal product in one area in order to maintain a competitive advantage in another."
   — *Ben Thompson, Stratechery* ([Strategy Credit](https://stratechery.com/2013/strategy-credit/))

   Running two world models creates the risk that product decisions for one model are constrained by the other — for example, not improving RTFM quality to avoid cannibalizing Marble revenue. This is a strategy tax. The product strategy must define clear boundaries: which dimensions each model owns, where they are allowed to overlap, and what happens when RTFM becomes "good enough" for current Marble use cases.

7. **The preview-to-production pattern is proven in creative tools**
   "Creatives don't want to wait. They want to see something immediately and then decide if it's worth refining."
   — *Scott Belsky, CPO of Adobe* ([SXSW 2024 Keynote](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=F0bMJI8KssI))

   Adobe's Firefly workflow is: generate fast (low-res preview) → iterate → commit to production quality. Figma's workflow is: design in low-fidelity wireframes → refine into high-fidelity mocks. The "explore fast, then render" pattern is native to how creatives work. RTFM → Marble is this pattern applied to 3D worlds. The product should make this feel like one continuous workflow, not two separate tools.

- Identify the **no-overlap zone** — capabilities only one model provides (e.g., only Marble exports GLB meshes; only RTFM runs in real-time)

### Step 2: Design Convergence Product Patterns

Produce a **Convergence Pattern Catalog** — 4-5 concrete UX architectures for how the two models compose into products.

Evaluate each pattern:

**Pattern A: Preview → Production**
- RTFM for fast interactive exploration ("draft mode"), Marble for high-fidelity render + export ("production mode")
- UX: User explores in real-time, finds the view/world they want, clicks "Render in high fidelity" → Marble generates the full persistent world
- Analogous to: Figma wireframe → Figma high-fidelity, RAW preview → Lightroom export
- Strength: Natural creative workflow. Clear upgrade path.
- Risk: Transition friction if the Marble output doesn't match the RTFM preview.

**Pattern B: Tiered Quality API**
- Single API endpoint with a `quality` parameter: `realtime` (RTFM) vs. `production` (Marble)
- UX: Developer chooses speed/quality tradeoff per request
- Analogous to: DALL-E quality parameter, video encoding presets
- Strength: Simple API surface, developer decides.
- Risk: Developers always choose cheapest option if quality difference is unclear.

**Pattern C: Progressive Refinement**
- Start with RTFM frame, progressively enhance to Marble quality while the user interacts
- UX: Instant low-quality view on load, quality improves over seconds/minutes as Marble generates
- Analogous to: Progressive JPEG loading, Google Maps tile loading
- Strength: Zero perceived latency with eventual high quality.
- Risk: Technically complex — requires handoff between two different 3D representations.

**Pattern D: Vertical-Specific Routing**
- Different verticals get different default models: gaming/VR gets RTFM (real-time matters), architecture/film gets Marble (export matters), robotics gets Marble (physics/collision matters)
- UX: Vertical-specific product surfaces, each optimized for that model
- Strength: Each vertical gets the best experience for their needs.
- Risk: Fragments the product and developer experience.

**Pattern E: Unified Spatial Canvas**
- Single product surface where RTFM powers navigation/exploration and Marble powers "save/export" of any view
- UX: Always exploring in RTFM. Pin a view → Marble renders it. Build a collection of high-fidelity views from an RTFM exploration session.
- Strength: Cleanest user mental model — one world, two resolution modes.
- Risk: Requires tight integration between RTFM spatial memory and Marble generation.

For each pattern, assess: technical feasibility, UX coherence, pricing implications, and which verticals it serves best. Recommend 1-2 patterns to pursue first with rationale.

**Output:** Convergence Pattern Catalog with 4-5 patterns, assessment matrix, and recommendation.

### Step 3: Design Multi-Model API Architecture

Produce a **Multi-Model API Design** showing how developers access both models through a coherent interface.

Consider:
- **One endpoint, model parameter:** `POST /worlds:generate` with `model: "rtfm"` or `model: "marble-plus"` or `model: "marble-mini"`
- **Separate endpoints:** `POST /worlds:explore` (RTFM) vs. `POST /worlds:generate` (Marble)
- **Session-based:** RTFM creates a persistent session (like a WebSocket), Marble creates a one-shot job (like current polling)

For each approach, evaluate:
- Developer ergonomics (how easy is it to understand and integrate?)
- Backward compatibility with existing World API consumers
- Pricing integration (how do credits work across models?)
- Streaming requirements (RTFM is real-time; does the API need WebSocket/SSE support?)
- State management (RTFM has spatial memory that persists during a session)

Document the recommended API surface with example requests/responses.

**Output:** Multi-Model API Design with endpoint specifications, example payloads, and migration path from current API.

### Step 4: Build Multi-Model Pricing Architecture

Produce a **Pricing Architecture** for a product with two models at different cost/value points.

- Read `context/products/world-api.md` for current Marble pricing ($1/1,250 credits, ~1,500 credits/world for plus, ~150 for mini)
- Design pricing that:
  - Incentivizes the right usage pattern (explore with RTFM, produce with Marble)
  - Doesn't create a perverse incentive to only use RTFM because it's cheaper
  - Accounts for RTFM's real-time nature (per-frame? per-session? per-minute?)
  - Works for both consumer (Marble app) and developer (API) surfaces

Pricing models to evaluate:

| Model | RTFM Pricing | Marble Pricing | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Unified credits** | X credits/minute of exploration | Y credits/world (current) | Simple, one credit pool | Hard to price real-time fairly |
| **Free preview, paid production** | Free (or very cheap) | Current pricing | Strong funnel, maximizes exploration | RTFM becomes a cost center |
| **Session-based** | Per-session flat fee | Per-world (current) | Predictable for users | Doesn't scale with usage |
| **Compute-based** | Per-GPU-second | Per-world (current) | Fair, transparent | Confusing for non-technical users |

Recommend a pricing architecture with rationale. Model the unit economics: what does an average RTFM session cost to serve (single H100, interactive framerates)?

**Output:** Pricing Architecture with tier design, credit structure, and unit economics model.

## Diagnostic Questions

1. **Does the team view RTFM and Marble as one product or two?** If the team sees them as separate products with separate roadmaps, the convergence strategy will fail. If they see them as two capabilities of one platform, convergence UX patterns become natural.

2. **What is RTFM's path from research preview to production API?** If RTFM remains a demo indefinitely, the convergence strategy is theoretical. The timeline for RTFM API availability determines the urgency of every recommendation in this skill.

3. **Can RTFM output seed a Marble generation?** This is the key technical question. If a user's RTFM exploration session can be "saved" and fed to Marble as a generation starting point, the Preview → Production pattern works. If not, the two models are disconnected.

4. **How fast is RTFM quality improving?** If RTFM quality is approaching Marble mini within 6 months, cannibalization is a real risk. If RTFM quality plateaus well below Marble, the tiered model is stable long-term.

5. **Which verticals have expressed interest in real-time generation?** If gaming studios are asking for real-time and architecture firms want high-fidelity export, the vertical routing pattern is natural. If all verticals want both, the unified canvas is the right approach.

6. **What does the RTFM compute cost profile look like?** If running RTFM for a 5-minute exploration session costs pennies, "free preview" pricing works. If it costs dollars, session-based pricing is necessary.

7. **Is there founder alignment on convergence vs. separation?** Fei-Fei Li's spatial intelligence framing suggests convergence (one platform). But research teams often prefer separation (distinct models, distinct papers). Understanding the internal dynamics informs how aggressively to push for unified product experiences.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Treating RTFM as "Marble Lite"** — RTFM is not a cheaper, faster version of Marble. It is a fundamentally different interaction model — continuous exploration vs. one-shot generation. Positioning it as "Marble but faster" undersells the experience shift and invites direct quality comparisons it will lose.

2. **Delaying convergence strategy until RTFM is "ready"** — By the time RTFM has a production API, the product architecture decisions (endpoints, pricing, UX patterns) need to already be designed. Waiting to plan convergence until both models are production-ready means shipping a disconnected experience and retrofitting integration later.

3. **Unified pricing that accidentally kills one model** — If RTFM exploration is priced per-frame at Marble rates, nobody will explore. If Marble is priced the same as RTFM, nobody will pay for high-fidelity. The pricing must create a natural flow from exploration (cheap/free) to production (premium).

4. **Building two separate product surfaces instead of one converged experience** — Two apps, two APIs, two dashboards = two products to maintain, two brands to explain, and user confusion about when to use which. The goal is one spatial intelligence platform with multiple capability modes.

5. **Ignoring the cannibalization question** — If RTFM quality improves 10x in a year, who still needs Marble? The honest answer might be "only users who need exportable 3D assets." Product strategy must account for a future where RTFM quality matches Marble for most visual use cases, and Marble's value is in geometry and export, not visual fidelity.

6. **Over-engineering the API before validating the UX pattern** — Design the user experience first (which convergence pattern works?), then design the API to support it. An API designed in a vacuum will constrain UX options later.
